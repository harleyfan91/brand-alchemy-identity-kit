# CTA Copywriting Rules — Brand Alchemy Reference

Established through research and revision, April–May 2026.
This document governs all deterministic CTA copy output across the Brand Identity Guide and related kit surfaces.

Every example in this document has been verified against every stated rule before publishing.
If a future example and a rule conflict, fix the example, not the rule.

---

## What this is

A set of explicit rules for writing CTAs that sound like a real brand, not a system.
The standard: would a competent branding agency or senior copywriter approve this line?
If the answer is no, rewrite it.

These rules apply to both the phrase banks and any new copy added to the system.

---

## 1. What a CTA is

A CTA is the line or button that asks the reader to do one specific thing next.
It is not an explanation of the product. It is not a description of what will happen.
It is a prompt that makes the next action feel obvious and easy.

One action per CTA. If a line is doing two things, split it or cut one.

Both lines must be paste-ready. A business owner should be able to copy either line directly
into a caption, button, email, or listing without editing. Nothing is instructional prose.
Nothing explains itself.

## 2. Tone rules

### The three tones

**bold:** Short declarative verbs. Confident. No hedging. Sentence fragments are acceptable and often
preferred. Reads like a brand that knows exactly who it's for and isn't apologizing for it.
- "See the work. Start a project."
- "DM 'QUOTE' and I'll get back to you today."

**friendly:** Warm and conversational. First-person singular or plural depending on the narrator.
Inviting without being soft. Reads like a competent person you'd actually want to work with.
- "Let us come take a look. Book online in two minutes."
- "Tell us what's going on and we'll figure it out together."

**professional:** Complete sentences. Specific next steps. Expectation-setting. Reads like a business
that takes its work seriously and respects the reader's time.
- "Request a complimentary project estimate. Written quotes provided within one business day."
- "Please reply with your project scope and timeline and we will respond within two business days."

### Tone never changes mid-entry

Both lines in a `[line1, line2]` pair must be in the same register.
Do not mix "Hey, DM us!" with "We will respond within one business day."

### Tone and narrator

For solo operators (solo_expert, solo_maker), use first-person singular:
"I'll get back to you," "I'll put together a quote."
For teams and organizations, use first-person plural: "We'll take it from there."
The phrase bank defaults to the most common narrator for each industry group.

## 3. Emotional register — the rule underneath all the other rules

This is the most important rule and the hardest to apply mechanically.
Every industry has a default emotional posture that its CTAs must communicate.
Getting the vocabulary right but the emotional register wrong produces copy that reads as
generic or, worse, off-putting. Both failures come from the wrong register:

- "Come in or order from home — either works." has the wrong register for hospitality.
  "Either works" communicates indifference. Hospitality copy communicates desire and invitation.
- "Send the brief — I'll tell you if I can help and what it would cost." has the wrong register
  for creative services. It positions the creative as a gatekeeper. Creative studio copy
  communicates collaborative curiosity and enthusiasm for the work.

### Emotional register by industry group

**trades_home:** Competence and ease. The reader has a problem; the business is the capable,
straightforward person who will handle it. No fuss, clear next step, honest pricing.
- Right: "Get a written quote in 24 hours."
- Wrong: "We'd love to help you explore your options."

**food_hospitality:** Desire and invitation. Lead with the pull of the experience, not the
logistics of how to access it. The CTA is an extension of the warmth that defines the business.
"We'd love to feed you" is right. "Either works" is wrong. "Whichever is convenient" is wrong.
The reader should feel wanted, not accommodated.
- Right: "Come in this week. We'd love to have you."
- Wrong: "You can dine in or order takeout, either option is available."

**retail_maker:** Discovery and craft. The reader is being invited into a world they'll want to
inhabit. The energy is about the thing itself — what it's made from, how it's made, why it's
worth having. Excitement is native here in a way it is not for professional services.
- Right: "Shop the collection. Everything is made by hand."
- Wrong: "You can purchase our products through the link."

**health_wellness:** Safety and clarity. The reader may be making a vulnerable decision.
No pressure, no false urgency, no feeling of being pushed. The business is a trustworthy guide,
not a salesperson. The first step should feel easy and low-risk.
- Right: "Book a free intro session. No commitment needed."
- Wrong: "Slots are filling fast — reserve yours today."

**creative_pro:** Collaborative curiosity. The reader has something they want to build.
The creative wants to hear about it. The posture is enthusiasm and partnership, not gatekeeping.
"Tell us about the project" is right. "I'll tell you if I can help" is wrong — it positions
the creative as evaluator rather than collaborator, which creates friction.
- Right: "Tell us about the project. We'd love to hear what you're working on."
- Wrong: "Send the brief and I'll tell you if I can help."

**professional_svc:** Clarity and trust. The reader is often anxious about something. The CTA
should reduce anxiety, not amplify it. Confidentiality, plain language, and a low-pressure first
step are the conversion drivers. Credibility over energy.
- Right: "Book a free consultation. All discussions are strictly confidential."
- Wrong: "Don't wait — schedule your call today!"

**community:** Belonging and purpose. The reader has a desire to contribute. The CTA should
honor that desire by making it easy and making them feel genuinely needed, not transactional.
- Right: "Come find out how you can help. There's something for everyone."
- Wrong: "Submit a volunteer application through the form below."

## 4. Platform rules

Every platform has a native register. Copy must match it or it reads foreign.

### Website

The reader chose to visit. CTAs can be slightly longer and more complete.
Action verb paired with a specific outcome or next step. No artificial urgency.
- "See recent projects and start a conversation."
- "Browse the full collection and place your order online."

### Email

Conversational and direct. Reply-trigger mechanics are native here: "Reply 'YES'," "Hit reply."
First-person throughout. Short sentences. The reader is already a warm contact; match that familiarity.
- "Reply with your job details and I'll send a quote by end of day."
- "Hit reply with a bit about your project and we'll figure out if we're a good fit."

### Social — casual (Instagram, TikTok, Facebook, Pinterest, Threads)

Platform vocabulary is mandatory. "Link in bio" not "click here." "DM" not "send me a message."
"Comment 'WORD'" is a native mechanic. Sentence fragments are normal here.
Energy should match the scroll context: punchy, human, no corporate stiffness.
- "DM 'QUOTE' and I'll get back to you today."
- "Tap the link and grab one while they're here."

### Social — professional (LinkedIn)

Longer, more complete sentences. Value-led framing before the ask. No DM-style triggers.
The ask comes after establishing context or credibility.
- "Working on a commercial project? DM us to discuss scope and timeline."
- "If you manage properties or run a construction business, let's connect."

### Directory — Google Business

Ultra-short. Native actions: Call, Message, Request info, Get Directions. One clear instruction.
No explanatory text. Read on mobile in seconds.
- "Call to schedule your free estimate."
- "Message us your job details. Quote in 24 hours."

### Directory — Yelp / TripAdvisor

Discovery mode. The reader is comparing options. No pressure. Lead with credibility or ease,
then direct to the next step.
- "Check our reviews and call when you're ready."
- "Tap 'Call' to reach us directly."

### Marketplace (Etsy / storefront)

Transactional register. Cart and shop language is native: "Add to cart," "Shop the collection,"
"Message the shop." Maker-culture warmth and craft references land here, not for trades or
professional services.

### Email — audience_growth and retention

Reply triggers are the primary mechanic: "Reply 'YES'," "Reply 'IN'."
Keep the ask minimal. Be explicit about frequency and what they're signing up for.
One sentence max on the frequency promise.

### Intentional platform-goal gaps (do not author CTAs for these combinations)

Several `(surface, goal)` combinations are deliberately empty in `CTA_PHRASE_BANKS.md`. Authoring CTAs for them would invent vocabulary the platform doesn't natively support and produce copy that fails the §13 "would a real business actually say this?" smell test.

| Gap | Why it's intentional |
|---|---|
| LinkedIn × `direct_sales` | LinkedIn is not a transactional surface. B2B SMBs use it for `lead_gen`, not "buy now" mechanics. |
| LinkedIn × `retention` | No native re-engage-past-customers mechanic on LinkedIn for SMB businesses. |
| Google Business × `audience_growth` | Google Business posts don't drive subscribers or followers. The platform has no "follow this business" mechanic equivalent to social. |
| Google Business × `retention` | The Google Business listing is a discovery surface, not a re-engagement surface. |
| Yelp / TripAdvisor × `lead_gen` | Yelp is a discovery/comparison surface only. No native lead-capture mechanic beyond Call/Website. |
| Yelp / TripAdvisor × `audience_growth` | Yelp doesn't host audience-growth mechanics. |
| Yelp / TripAdvisor × `retention` | Yelp doesn't host retention mechanics. |

Locked decision: [`CTA_BANK_AUDIT.md`](../../docs/research/CTA_BANK_AUDIT.md) §4B, May 26 2026. When a buyer's `primaryGoal` lands on one of these absent intersections, the deterministic surface router in [`OUTPUT_TRANSLATION_SPEC.md`](../../../OUTPUT_TRANSLATION_SPEC.md) §10A.6A should route to a different (native) surface for that goal. Falling through to `ctaTemplates` for these combinations is a known degraded state. See `CTA_BANK_AUDIT.md` §4B.1 for the upstream routing work item.

## 5. Industry variance rules

CTAs must sound like the business, not like the system that generated them.
The test: if a local plumber reads their own CTA, does it sound like a plumber wrote it?
If a maker reads theirs, does it sound like a maker wrote it?

See section 3 for the emotional register that governs each group. The vocabulary below
is the surface expression of that register, not a substitute for it.

### trades_home (construction, home services, automotive)

Vocabulary: estimate, quote, site visit, on-site, schedule, available, local, licensed, insured.
Mechanics: call, message, book, fill out the form.
Free estimate is the primary lead mechanic. Availability, not urgency.
- "Get a written quote in 24 hours."
- "DM your address and what needs doing. I'll get you a quote."

### food_hospitality (food, beverage, hospitality)

Vocabulary: reserve, book, menu, specials, in-house, open, pickup, walk-ins, come in.
Mechanics: reserve online, call ahead, order online, link in bio.
Never "either works." Never lead with logistics. Lead with desire and invitation.
- "Come in this week. We'd love to have you."
- "Follow us if you want to know what's on before you arrive."

### retail_maker (retail, Etsy, marketplace)

Vocabulary: shop, collection, drop, batch, handmade, made by hand, ships, add to cart.
Mechanics: tap the link, shop the drop, comment 'WANT IT', message the shop.
Small-batch scarcity is earned here if factually true. Exclamation marks appear here.
- "Tap the link to shop! Ships in three days, made by hand."
- "Comment 'WANT IT' and I'll DM the link!"

### health_wellness (health, wellness, beauty, fitness)

Vocabulary: book, appointment, session, consult, free intro, availability, slots, morning/evening.
Mechanics: book online, link in bio, reply to book, call.
Low-pressure, safety-first language. No manufactured urgency.
- "Book a free intro session. No commitment needed."
- "Find a time and lock it in. Takes about a minute."

### creative_pro (creative services, photography, design, education)

Vocabulary: portfolio, project, brief, scope, proposal, booking, inquiry, discovery call.
Mechanics: view the portfolio, DM the brief, submit an inquiry, tell us about the project.
Collaborative and curious, not evaluative. Never "I'll tell you if I can help."
- "Tell us about the project. We'd love to hear what you're working on."
- "See the work, then let's talk."

### professional_svc (legal, finance, consulting, real estate)

Vocabulary: consultation, complimentary, confidential, initial conversation, next steps, situation.
Mechanics: schedule, book, reply to arrange, call.
No pressure, no exclamation marks, no urgency language. Clarity and trust.
- "Book a free consultation today. Clear answers, no runaround."
- "Schedule a complimentary consultation. All discussions are strictly confidential."

### community (nonprofits, community organizations)

Vocabulary: volunteer, get involved, give, support, donate, partner, contribute, join.
Mechanics: tap the link, DM, reply, sign up.
Belonging and genuine warmth. The reader's desire to help is honored, not processed.
- "Come find out how you can help. There's something for everyone."
- "Follow along and see the work we do together."

## 6. Brevity rules

### Line 1

Aim for 10 words or fewer. Hard cap at 15. If it's longer, cut or split.

### Line 2

Same length or shorter than line 1. It is a second CTA or a single supporting statement.
It is never an explanation of line 1. It is never instructional prose. See section 11.

### List signup lines (do not promote opting out)

Paste-ready CTAs for **follow, subscribe, sign up, join the list** should **sell the value and cadence**
of staying on the list (what they get, how often, why it is worth opening).

Do **not** use line 2 (or line 1) to highlight **unsubscribe, opt-out, leave anytime,** or similar.
That frames the relationship as easy to exit instead of worth entering. It also competes with the job
of the CTA, which is to earn the signup.

**Unsubscribe links, preference centers, and legally required opt-out copy** still belong in the real
touchpoints: email footer, signup disclaimer, terms, or the first automated message. This rule is
only about what we put in **marketing CTA lines** in the phrase bank.

### Cut filler

| Too long or wrong | Tighter |
|---|---|
| "Come in or order from home, either works." | "Come in this week. We'd love to have you." |
| "Send the brief and I'll tell you if I can help." | "Tell us about the project. We'd love to hear it." |
| "You can add this to cart right from the listing." | "Add to cart. Ships in three days." |
| "When you're ready, you can book a short intro in a couple of clicks." | "Book a free intro session. No commitment needed." |
| "A quick note through the contact form is enough to get started." | "Send us a message. We'll take it from there." |

## 7. Punctuation rules

### Em dash (—) — banned

Em dashes are not used anywhere in CTA copy. This is a clean rule with no exceptions.

The reason is not that em dashes are grammatically wrong. The reason is that in short punchy
CTA lines, a period almost always produces better rhythm, and avoiding the em dash entirely
removes the judgment call about when it is and isn't acceptable. That judgment call is where
contradictions keep appearing. A period is always right.

Before/after for every former em dash use:
- "Message us your job details. Quote in 24 hours." (not "— quote in 24 hours")
- "DM your address and what needs doing. I'll get you a quote." (not "— I'll get you")
- "Find a time and lock it in. Takes about a minute." (not "— takes about a minute")
- "Or call. We pick up." (not "— we pick up")
- "Small batch. When it's gone, it's gone." (not "— when it's gone")
- "Come in this week. We'd love to have you." (not "— either works")

In prose-length explanatory text (like this rules document), em dashes are acceptable.
In CTA copy lines, they are not.

### Exclamation marks

An exclamation mark must be earned by the energy of the line itself, not added to inject
energy that is not already there.

Earned contexts in this bank:
- retail_maker drops on casual social: "Comment 'WANT IT' and I'll DM the link!" — the mechanic has genuine energy
- community volunteer asks on casual social bold/friendly: "Tap the link to get involved!" — warmth and enthusiasm are native
- health_wellness booking on casual social lead_gen bold: "DM 'START' for a free intro session!" — booking momentum
- food/hospitality audience_growth on casual social bold: "Follow for daily specials, events, and behind-the-scenes!" — daily update culture
- community email bold: "Reply 'YES' and I'll add you to the volunteer list!" — reply trigger with warmth

Never acceptable:
- professional_svc, any surface, any tone
- professional tone, any industry, any surface
- LinkedIn, any surface, any industry
- Directory (Google Business, Yelp), any surface, any industry
- "now!" on any plain action verb: "Order online now!" "Book now!" "Shop now!" — not earned

"Shop now" as a two-word standalone button label is an industry convention and acceptable
without an exclamation mark. It is not used as a full-line CTA in this bank.

One per entry maximum. Never two exclamation marks in the same two-line entry.

### Periods

Used at the end of complete standalone sentences and sentence fragments as normal.

The staccato period style (short fragments separated by periods) is intentional for
parallel-beat constructions with three or more items:
- "Work. Process. Lessons learned." — three items, deliberate rhythm

For two parallel fragments, use a comma, not two periods:
- "No surprises, no pressure." not "No surprises. No pressure."
- "Local, licensed, insured." not "Local. Licensed. Insured."
- "Fast response, no obligation." not "Fast response. No obligation."
- "Real jobs, real results." not "Real jobs. Real results."

The test: does a period between two fragments create a meaningful pause you'd hear if the line
were read aloud? Or does it just chop the sentence in half? If it's the latter, use a comma.

Note: two short complete sentences separated by a period are different from two parallel
fragments. "Book a free intro session. No commitment needed." is two sentences, each complete.
That is correct. "No surprises. No pressure." is two parallel fragments that read better with
a comma.

### Commas

Default separator for two parallel fragments and supporting clauses.
- "No surprises, no pressure."
- "We get back to everyone fast, usually the same day."
- "Call or message, we're happy to help with an estimate."

Oxford comma always in lists of three or more items when written as prose.
In short modifier stacks used as labels, "and" is dropped: "Local, licensed, insured."

### Colons

Acceptable for introducing a list or a specific mechanism:
- "Sign up for our newsletter: launches, case studies, and process notes."
- "Reply with the basics: what it is, when you need it, what you've got so far."

### Question marks

Effective for opening a two-line entry in bold and friendly tones.
A question that mirrors the reader's situation creates immediate relevance before the ask.
- "Planning an event? Tell us the details."
- "Want something custom? Tell us."
- "Been a while? We've missed you."

### Quotation marks

Used for reply triggers only. The quoted word is the trigger the reader types.
Always single quotes, always capitalized: Reply 'YES', Reply 'IN', Comment 'HELP', DM 'QUOTE'.

## 8. Vocabulary rules

### Use

- Specific action verbs: book, reserve, order, shop, call, message, reply, DM, tap, follow,
  subscribe, send, submit, request, reach out, get in touch, sign up, drop a DM
- Industry-native words: see section 5. "Estimate" for trades, "session" for wellness,
  "drop" for retail makers, "brief" for creative pros, "consultation" for professional services
- Expectation-setting language: "usually same day," "within one business day,"
  "complimentary," "no obligation," "free to ask questions"
- Specificity: "within 24 hours" beats "quickly." "One email a month" beats "occasional updates."
- Second-person: "your project," "what you need," "what you're working on"
- Contractions: always, on all tones except the most formal professional entries

### Avoid

- "Now" as an intensifier on a routine action: "Order online now," "Book now."
  Exception: "Shop now" as a two-word standalone button label only, never with an exclamation mark.
- "Get started": vague, overused. Replace with the specific action.
- "Learn more": equally vague. Replace with what they'll actually find.
- "Click here": accessibility-poor and dated on every platform.
- "Either works," "whichever is convenient," "whatever you prefer": communicates indifference.
  These shrugs are particularly damaging in food/hospitality where warmth is the conversion driver.
- "I'll tell you if I can help": wrong register for creative_pro. Signals gatekeeping, not partnership.
- Fabricated urgency: "Don't miss out," "limited time," "only X left," "hurry."
- Hollow enthusiasm: "Excited to share," "we're thrilled," "delighted to announce."
- Business jargon: "solutions," "leverage," "synergy," "optimize," "seamless," "robust."
- Business-speak: "touch base," "circle back," "loop in."
- Empty intensifiers: "personalized support," "amazing results," "next level."
- System vocabulary: "touchpoint," "rollout," "first surface," "off-brand," "quick-start,"
  "deliverable," "rubric," "intake."

## 9. Scarcity and urgency rules

Urgency must be earned, not manufactured.

Acceptable because factually grounded:
- Small-batch product language for retail_maker: "Small batch. When it's gone, it's gone."
  This describes the business model accurately.
- Availability statements: "Same-week slots open," "Booking fast for this weekend." Factual when true.
- Booking windows: "Now booking Q3 and Q4." Describes availability state, not pressure.

Not acceptable anywhere:
- Countdown language: "Only 3 spots left," "Ends Friday," "Limited time offer."
- Manufactured FOMO: "Everyone is doing it," "Don't be left behind."
- Fake social proof: "Thousands of happy customers are waiting."

For professional_svc and health_wellness: urgency language is never appropriate regardless
of framing. These industries build trust through clarity, not pressure.

## 10. The professional_svc exception

professional_svc (legal, financial, consulting, real estate) is the only group where:

- No exclamation marks, ever, on any surface, in any tone
- No urgency or scarcity language, ever, on any surface
- Bold means direct and clear, not high-energy. A bold professional_svc entry reads more like
  a friendly entry in other industries.
- The first consultation being free or complimentary is always stated explicitly.
  It is the primary friction reducer for this audience.
- Confidentiality is mentioned whenever context allows.

These rules reflect the compliance posture appropriate for businesses that carry professional
liability or handle sensitive client information. They are not stylistic preferences.

## 11. The goal-shaping principle

The primary goal shapes the type of action asked for, not just tone or vocabulary.

| Goal | What the CTA asks for |
|---|---|
| direct_sales | The transaction: buy, book, reserve, order |
| lead_gen | Top of funnel: share details, start a conversation, book a free consult |
| audience_growth | Low-commitment relationship: follow, subscribe, sign up |
| retention | Re-engagement: come back, rebook, pick up where we left off |

A direct_sales CTA that says "follow us for updates" is wrong.
A lead_gen CTA that says "add to cart" is wrong.
The goal gates the vocabulary and the mechanics. This is non-negotiable.

## 12. Line 2 rules

Line 2 is not an explanation of line 1. It is one of the following:

A second CTA — a different action the reader could take:
- Line 1: "Book your free estimate today."
- Line 2: "Or call. We pick up."

A credibility or ease signal — something that reduces friction without being a sales pitch:
- Line 1: "Reserve your session online."
- Line 2: "Evening and weekend appointments available."

A frequency or expectation promise — for audience_growth entries specifically:
- Line 1: "Sign up for one practical tip a week."
- Line 2: "Real advice, no upsell."

Not an opt-out pitch: do not use line 2 to mention unsubscribing or leaving the list. See **section 6 — List signup lines (do not promote opting out).**

A warmth signal — for friendly tone entries where a human note closes naturally:
- Line 1: "Come in this week."
- Line 2: "We'd love to have you."

Line 2 almost never starts with "You can..." — that construction is slow and unnecessary.
"You can add this to cart right from the listing." becomes "Add to cart. Ships in three days."

## 13. What "sounds like a brand" means in practice

Read every line aloud. Does it sound like a person wrote it?
Does it sound like the specific kind of business it's supposed to be?

Four checks:
- Would a local plumber actually say this? Would a neighborhood restaurant post this?
- Is there any word here that the business owner would never use themselves?
- Does it feel like a template with blanks filled in, or does it feel specific?
- If you removed the industry and context, could this line be from anyone? If yes, not specific enough.

The goal of this system is to hand a non-marketer the copy a senior brand copywriter would
have written for their specific business. Every entry should clear that bar.

## Appendix: No-punctuation CTA lines (research note)

**Status:** documentation only. **Not implemented** as a systematic pattern in [`CTA_PHRASE_BANKS.md`](./CTA_PHRASE_BANKS.md) until a future authoring wave explicitly adopts it.

**Finding:** Common product-writing guidance treats **short UI control labels** (buttons, chips) as distinct from **sentence-length marketing lines**. Many design systems recommend **no terminal punctuation** on button labels because the control already signals closure (see [PatternFly — Punctuation — Buttons](https://www.patternfly.org/ux-writing/punctuation)). Microsoft’s style guide similarly avoids trailing punctuation when referring to UI option labels in procedures ([Formatting text in instructions](https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/style-guide/procedures-instructions/formatting-text-in-instructions)).

**Phrase bank default:** Paste-ready lines in this repo are authored primarily as **complete phrases or sentences** with normal punctuation for rhythm and clarity. An audit found **no** tuple lines that omitted punctuation entirely while matching the rest of the bank’s conventions.

**Optional future lane:** Ultra-short imperatives (often 1–4 words) may omit terminal punctuation when they read as **native button/chip copy** (e.g. “Book now”, “Get a quote”). That lane remains **research-backed but unimplemented** here to avoid mixing label grammar with caption/email grammar without an explicit policy pass.
